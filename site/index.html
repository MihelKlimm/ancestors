How the sub‑branch was resolved. The paternal call comes from yhaplo (23andMe’s open‑source caller) walking the full ISOGG tree across 3,741 Y markers. yhaplo expects GRCh37 while this file reports Y positions on GRCh38, so every coordinate was lifted with the UCSC hg38→hg19 chain rather than shifted by a constant — the offset is not constant (2,111,934 at one marker, 2,111,880 at another). It was checked against a known address: M170 lands on 14,847,792, exactly its dbSNP GRCh37 position, and 0 of 3,741 markers failed to map.

The call is I2a1b (I‑M423) on four derived markers, with the northwest‑European I2a2/M223 branch positively excluded by six ancestral markers — not merely unobserved. One limit remains: CTS10228, the marker of the Slavic “Din” expansion, is not assayed on this chip at all — zero calls either way. M423 is settled; whether it sits in the large Slavic sub‑branch or the small British “Isles” one is not.

What this is not. This is not an admixture estimate. Percentages like “38% Baltic, 12% Finnish” require comparing you against reference population panels, which this file alone cannot support — so none appear here. Haplogroups also trace only two lines out of the thousands of ancestors you had 300 years ago: your mother’s mother’s mother, and your father’s father’s father. They are precise, and they are narrow.
